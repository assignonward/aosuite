Even though business is nominally conducted in the open today, the cost of collecting detailed information about business operations is prohibitively high for all but the business operators. Limited information, like sitting in a drug store parking lot counting the number of customers entering the store, is available and sufficient for competitors to estimate the viability of opening a store nearby, but it is not detailed or complete in the way that an open record could potentially be. Like the transformation in real estate valuation when county record books went online, the cost of collection and analysis of detailed trends will drop dramatically as more and more business information becomes publicly available, whether through the business operators' records, or their customers'.

Obfuscation

In the 2011 timeframe, popular (incorrect) opinion held that bitcoin transactions were anonymous. What they were in reality was obscure, difficult (at the time) to trace, but far from impossible. After a few well publicized arrests of bitcoin based criminals, new protocols were developed to intentionally "mix" transactions to make them even more difficult to trace. Similar to
*and sometimes used in conjunction with
the TOR network protocol, transactions are broken up and mixed with others with intermediate records discarded, hoping to break the linkage between the parties doing the transaction, but... unless both parties do a large volume of business with a large number of other parties over the network and are patient enough to wait a long time for their obfuscated transaction to complete, Alice sending Bob $100 at a certain time will always show up as Alice sending $100 and Bob receiving $100 around the same time.

Regulation

Imperfect as they may be at keeping transactions completely anonymous, and as many legitimate potential uses as they may have, obfuscation protocols are also undeniably useful money laundering tools - and as such they are early targets for regulation and outright legal bans. Open, fully transparent protocols may, conversely, be viewed as a positive change by regulators, taxing authorities, etc.

Non legally binding credit

The chief concern of most issuers of credit is the ability, and willingness, of the recipient to repay the loan, on the agreed upon terms. The current legal system recognizes contracts between competent parties, individuals of legal age and corporations. When a debtor fails to repay their creditor on terms agreed to in a legally binding document the courts will enforce repayment of the debt through property liens, wage garnishment and even imprisonment of the debtor. In the Island Life story, Eddie extends non legally binding credit to Bob. Eddie knows, based on his observations of Bob's cash flow, that Bob is able to repay the loan in a timely fashion, and based on his personal relationship with Bob he knows that Bob will honor the debt. Since the debt is recorded on Bob's active blockchain, Bob is able to repay it in installments: one plate of curry goat at a time. Eddie resells the debt to Charlie who is in a better position to market it one plate at a time to the tourists he brings from the cruise ship. This starts as an act of faith in Bob by Eddie, then Charlie, and then as simple transactions between Charlie and Bob's customers who are buying one BCG at a time from Charlie and trading them immediately to Bob for a plate of curry goat.

But I don't Like it!

Like it or not, technology is bringing transparency to modern life. It has been ever since the invention of writing, and continues through to the ubquitous handheld video recorder. Machine vision / object recognition, automatic readers, cheap terabyte storage devices, instant global broadband communication... all these things are accelerating transparency in daily life at an alarming rate. You may choose to keep your financial transactions private, use cash rather than obviously traceable credit cards, calculate your taxes with pencil and paper instead of using the big centralized online accounting apps - but the evidence shows that if you are doing these things in an attempt to protect your financial privacy: A) you are in the minority, and B) it's probably not as effective as you think it is.

Politicians are unlikely to get elected on "privacy stripping" platforms, so cash is unlikely to go away any time soon, but there are costs associated with cash transactions, consumers have to obtain cash to spend, and businesses have to collect, protect and deposit the cash with banks. Credit cards collect an obvious tax on every transaction they process, even if they do try to hide this from consumers. It wouldn't make a lot of sense to transfer title to a family home on a web of trust based blockchain, at least not in today's world, but a sandwich? Or a bag of groceries? This is where the web of trust can start, small low risk transactions, and as a history of transparent transactions accumulate, those who have been transparent in their dealings can start to enjoy relief from the credit card and cash overhead, just like credit card users enjoy better credit ratings than cash only people and businesses.
